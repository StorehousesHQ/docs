## Overview

Track your historical and cultural artifact collection with detailed fields for origin culture, period, material, and provenance. Perfect for collectors, institutions, and enthusiasts managing archaeological items, antiquities, and cultural objects.

<Card title="Artifact Tracking Features" icon="landmark">
  - Origin culture and period/era tracking
  - Material and artifact type classification
  - Condition assessment
  - Provenance documentation
  - Legal compliance notes
  - Market value tracking
</Card>

## Adding an Artifact

<Steps>
  <Step title="Navigate to Add Item">
    Click "Add Item" from your dashboard or go to [storehouses.app/add](https://storehouses.app/add)
  </Step>

  <Step title="Select Historical & Cultural Artifacts">
    Choose "Historical & Cultural Artifacts" as the category
  </Step>

  <Step title="Enter Details">
    Fill in origin culture, period, artifact type, material, and condition
  </Step>

  <Step title="Add Documentation">
    Upload photos, provenance documents, and export/import paperwork
  </Step>

  <Step title="Save">
    Click "Save Item" to add to your collection
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Artifact Fields

### Origin Information

<ParamField path="origin_culture" type="string">
  The culture, civilization, or region of origin

  **Examples:**
  - Ancient Egyptian
  - Roman Empire
  - Ming Dynasty Chinese
  - Pre-Columbian Maya
  - Ancient Greek
  - Byzantine
  - Mesopotamian
  - Native American
  - African (specify tribe/region)
  - Medieval European

  <Tip>
    Be as specific as possible about the cultural origin
  </Tip>
</ParamField>

<ParamField path="period_era" type="string">
  Time period or era of the artifact

  **Examples:**
  - New Kingdom (1550-1070 BCE)
  - 1st Century CE
  - Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE)
  - Medieval (500-1500 CE)
  - Iron Age
  - Bronze Age
  - Classical Period
  - Hellenistic Period
  - Renaissance

  **Include:**
  - Approximate date range when known
  - Archaeological period designations
  - Dynasty or reign when applicable
</ParamField>

### Physical Description

<ParamField path="artifact_type" type="string">
  Classification of the artifact

  **Categories:**

  **Pottery & Ceramics**
  - Vessels (amphorae, urns, vases)
  - Figurines
  - Tiles
  - Architectural elements

  **Stone & Sculpture**
  - Statuary
  - Relief carvings
  - Tools
  - Architectural fragments

  **Metalwork**
  - Jewelry
  - Weapons
  - Tools
  - Coins
  - Vessels

  **Textiles**
  - Garments
  - Tapestries
  - Fragments
  - Burial textiles

  **Other**
  - Glassware
  - Bone/Ivory
  - Wood
  - Papyrus/Documents
</ParamField>

<ParamField path="material" type="string">
  Primary material(s) of the artifact

  **Examples:**
  - Terracotta
  - Bronze
  - Marble
  - Gold
  - Limestone
  - Jade
  - Faience
  - Iron
  - Obsidian
  - Ivory
  - Linen
  - Papyrus

  <Note>
    List multiple materials if the artifact is composite (e.g., "Bronze with gold inlay")
  </Note>
</ParamField>

<ParamField path="condition" type="select">
  Overall condition of the artifact

  **Standard grading:**
  - **Excellent** - Minimal wear, original surface intact
  - **Very Good** - Minor wear, no significant damage
  - **Good** - Some wear or damage, structurally sound
  - **Fair** - Notable damage or restoration
  - **Poor** - Significant damage, fragmentary

  **Condition notes should include:**
  - Original vs. restored elements
  - Surface condition
  - Any repairs or conservation
  - Missing elements
  - Structural integrity
</ParamField>

### Dimensions

<ParamField path="dimensions" type="string">
  Physical measurements

  **Format examples:**
  - "Height: 15 cm, Width: 8 cm"
  - "12 x 8 x 4 inches"
  - "Diameter: 25 cm"
  - "Length: 45 cm, fragmentary"

  <Tip>
    Use consistent units (metric preferred for antiquities)
  </Tip>
</ParamField>

### Provenance

<ParamField path="provenance" type="textarea">
  Documented ownership history

  **Include:**
  - Previous owners with dates
  - Auction or dealer sales
  - Exhibition history
  - Publication references
  - Import/export documentation
  - Archaeological find information (if known)

  **Example:**
  ```
  Ex-collection Baron von Schmidt, Munich (acquired 1920s)
  Christie's London, June 1985, lot 234
  Private collection, New York, 1985-2020
  Acquired from XYZ Gallery, 2020
  ```

  <Warning>
    Provenance is critical for legal compliance and authenticity. Document thoroughly.
  </Warning>
</ParamField>

## Legal Considerations

<Warning>
  **Important Legal Notice**: The antiquities market is heavily regulated. Always ensure your artifacts have legal provenance and comply with cultural property laws.
</Warning>

### Documentation Requirements

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Provenance Documentation" icon="file-contract">
    Maintain records showing:
    - Ownership history (ideally pre-1970)
    - Import/export documentation
    - Customs declarations
    - Purchase receipts and invoices
    - Auction catalogs and records
    - Previous owner correspondence

    **Why 1970 matters:** The 1970 UNESCO Convention is the standard cutoff for establishing legal provenance
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Export/Import Papers" icon="passport">
    Required documentation may include:
    - Export license from country of origin
    - Import declaration
    - Customs paperwork
    - CITES permits (for ivory, etc.)
    - Cultural property certificates

    <Tip>
      Keep all customs and import paperwork with your records
    </Tip>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Authentication" icon="certificate">
    Consider obtaining:
    - Thermoluminescence (TL) dating for ceramics
    - Scientific analysis reports
    - Expert authentication letters
    - Comparative scholarly references
    - Museum or academic opinions
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Best Practices

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Document Provenance Thoroughly" icon="link">
    The most critical aspect of artifact collecting:
    - Document every ownership transfer
    - Keep all receipts and invoices
    - Photograph items with dated newspapers
    - Maintain export/import paperwork
    - Record publication and exhibition history

    **Why:** Provenance establishes legality and authenticity, affecting both value and ability to sell
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Photograph Comprehensively" icon="camera">
    Capture detailed photos of:
    - Multiple angles (front, back, sides, top, bottom)
    - Surface details and patina
    - Any inscriptions or markings
    - Damage or repairs
    - Scale reference (ruler in photo)
    - Any associated documentation

    <Tip>
      Good documentation photos help with insurance claims and authentication
    </Tip>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Store Properly" icon="shield">
    Protect your artifacts:
    - Control temperature and humidity
    - Avoid direct sunlight
    - Use acid-free materials
    - Support fragile items properly
    - Secure storage for valuables
    - Handle with clean cotton gloves

    **Climate recommendations:**
    - Temperature: 65-70°F (18-21°C)
    - Humidity: 45-55% relative humidity
    - Stable conditions (avoid fluctuations)
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Get Expert Opinions" icon="user-graduate">
    For valuable or significant pieces:
    - Consult specialists in the culture/period
    - Obtain authentication letters
    - Consider scientific testing
    - Compare with museum examples
    - Research published parallels

    <Note>
      Expert documentation adds value and confidence
    </Note>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Insurance Considerations" icon="shield-check">
    For valuable artifacts:
    - Get specialized fine art insurance
    - Obtain professional appraisals
    - Document thoroughly with photos
    - Update values periodically
    - Consider scheduled coverage for high-value items

    <Tip>
      Export your artifact inventory from Storehouses for insurance documentation
    </Tip>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Stay Informed on Laws" icon="gavel">
    Cultural property laws change:
    - Follow AAMD guidelines
    - Monitor bilateral agreements
    - Understand UNESCO conventions
    - Know your country's import laws
    - Consult legal counsel for major purchases
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Common Questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How do I verify authenticity?" icon="search">
    **Authentication methods:**
    - Expert examination by specialists
    - Scientific testing (TL, C14, XRF analysis)
    - Stylistic comparison with known examples
    - Provenance research
    - Museum or academic consultation

    <Warning>
      Be cautious of artifacts with no provenance history - forgeries are common
    </Warning>
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What is the 1970 rule?" icon="calendar">
    **The 1970 UNESCO Convention:**
    - Establishes international standards for cultural property
    - Many institutions and dealers require provenance before 1970
    - Artifacts with documented pre-1970 provenance are more desirable
    - Some countries have earlier cutoff dates

    **Best practice:** Seek artifacts with documented ownership history prior to 1970
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How do I value artifacts?" icon="dollar-sign">
    **Valuation factors:**
    - Provenance quality and documentation
    - Rarity and artistic quality
    - Condition
    - Cultural and historical significance
    - Market demand
    - Authentication documentation

    **Valuation resources:**
    - Auction results (Christie's, Sotheby's, Bonhams)
    - Specialist dealer pricing
    - Professional appraisals
    - Insurance valuations
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I sell artifacts internationally?" icon="globe">
    **Depends on:**
    - Country of origin export laws
    - Your country's import/export regulations
    - UNESCO agreements
    - Specific artifact type (some materials restricted)
    - Documentation quality

    **Always:**
    - Consult with legal counsel
    - Obtain necessary permits
    - Document provenance thoroughly
    - Work with reputable auction houses or dealers
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Should I clean or restore artifacts?" icon="spray-can">
    **Generally, no:**
    - Original patina is valuable
    - Amateur cleaning can damage items
    - Restoration affects value
    - Conservation should be done by professionals only

    <Tip>
      Dust carefully with soft brush only. For anything more, consult a conservator.
    </Tip>
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
